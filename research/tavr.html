Real-time predictive modeling of TAVR deployment mechanics via reduced order modeling — enabling patient-specific pre-procedural risk assessment.

Overview

Transcatheter Aortic Valve Replacement (TAVR) is a minimally invasive procedure for treating severe aortic stenosis, increasingly used in patients across all surgical risk profiles. Despite its success, TAVR carries procedure-specific risks — including coronary obstruction, paravalvular leakage, and conduction abnormalities — that depend critically on patient-specific anatomy and device selection.

My work focuses on developing a reduced order model (ROM)-driven computational framework that enables real-time, patient-specific mechanical simulations of TAVR deployment. By drastically reducing the computational cost of high-fidelity finite element simulations — from hours to milliseconds — this framework opens the door to genuine pre-procedural planning in the clinic.

Simulation of TAVR stent deployment mechanics — reduced order model prediction of stent deformation in patient-specific aortic geometry.

Methodology

How it works

01
High-Fidelity Full Order Model (FOM) Patient-specific aortic geometries are reconstructed from CT imaging. Full finite element simulations of stent deployment are run using contact mechanics to generate a snapshot library of deformation states.
02
Reduced Basis Construction via POD Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) is applied to the snapshot library to extract the dominant modes of structural deformation, constructing a low-dimensional reduced basis.
03
Reduced Order Model Assembly The governing equations are projected onto the reduced basis, yielding a dramatically smaller system that retains the essential physics while achieving 99% faster solve times.
04
Real-Time Patient-Specific Prediction For a new patient anatomy, the ROM provides instantaneous mechanical predictions — stent deformation, contact forces, and coronary obstruction risk — enabling pre-procedural planning in a clinical setting.

Transparent view of stent deployment showing internal contact mechanics and radial expansion within the aortic root.

Impact

The framework has been validated against full-order simulations and demonstrates strong agreement while achieving computational speedups exceeding 99%. A patent has been filed on the core methodology, and ongoing work is extending the framework to predict coronary obstruction risk in real time — a clinically critical outcome that currently has no reliable pre-procedural computational tool.

This work was supported by the American Heart Association Predoctoral Fellowship and presented at TCT 2024 and TCT 2025, the premier interventional cardiology conferences.
