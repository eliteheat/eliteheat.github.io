Coronary

Coronary Stent
Hemodynamics

Investigating the effects of drug-eluting stent platforms on endothelial wall shear stress in patient-specific coronary arteries — from the prospective SHEAR-STENT clinical trial.

Overview

Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with drug-eluting stents (DES) is the most common treatment for obstructive coronary artery disease. Despite excellent acute results, in-stent restenosis and late stent thrombosis remain significant concerns, driven in part by the local hemodynamic environment created by the stent platform.

My work on this project focuses on computational fluid dynamics analysis of patient-specific coronary arteries from the SHEAR-STENT clinical trial — a prospective randomized study comparing the Medtronic Resolute and Abbott Xience drug-eluting stent platforms. By reconstructing post-stenting coronary geometries from intravascular imaging and running high-fidelity CFD simulations, we quantify the effect of stent footprint geometry on endothelial wall shear stress (WSS), a key biomechanical driver of neointimal healing and restenosis.

Overview of framework: patient-specific coronary geometry reconstruction from intravascular imaging, CFD mesh generation, and wall shear stress analysis comparing Xience and Resolute stent platforms.

Methodology

How it works

01
Patient-Specific Geometry Reconstruction Post-stenting coronary artery geometries are reconstructed from optical coherence tomography (OCT) and intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) imaging using custom segmentation pipelines.
02
Stent Footprint Modeling The stent strut geometry for both the Xience and Resolute platforms is incorporated into the coronary lumen model, capturing the local flow disturbances created by each stent design.
03
CFD Simulation & WSS Analysis High-fidelity steady and pulsatile CFD simulations are run using ANSYS Fluent. Wall shear stress maps are computed and compared across stent platforms and clinical outcomes.
04
Clinical Correlation Computational WSS results are correlated with 12-month neointimal healing outcomes from OCT follow-up imaging in the SHEAR-STENT trial, linking biomechanics to clinical endpoints.

Impact

This work has resulted in multiple peer-reviewed publications and conference presentations, including papers in Computer Methods and Programs in Biomedicine and Cardiovascular Revascularization Medicine. The findings provide mechanistic insight into how stent platform design affects the local hemodynamic environment — with direct implications for DES selection and future stent design optimization.

A related study leveraging ultrafast Doppler intravascular ultrasound for real-time WSS imaging in stented arteries has recently been accepted in Scientific Reports, representing a novel approach to in vivo hemodynamic assessment.
